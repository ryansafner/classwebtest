Getting Set Up

Before we begin, start a new file with \(\rightarrow\) \(\rightarrow\) . As you work through this sheet in the console in R, also add (copy/paste) your commands that work into this new file. At the end, save it, and run to execute all of your commands at once.

Create a vector called “x” with all the even integers from 2 to 10.

3.

Find the mean of x with mean()

4.

Now take the following pdf of random variable \(Y\):

y p(y)
2 0.50
4 0.25
6 0.25

Calculate the standard deviation “manually” using our table method. You can look at the source code of Lecture 4 for my example.

    1. Create two vectors, one called \(y.i\) and one called \(p.i\), with the data above.
    1. Merge them into a data frame called \(rv\) with data.frame(y_i,p_i). Call rv to inspect it.
    1. Find the expected value of \(Y\) by taking the sum of each value of y.i multiplied by p.i with the sum() command.
    1. Create a new column in rv called deviations, where you subtract the mean from each y.i value. Call rv again to make sure it’s now there.
    1. Create another column in rv called devsq, where you square the deviations from part d. Call rv again to make sure it’s now there.
    1. Now add another column in rv called weighteddevsq, where you multiply the squared deviations in part e. by the associated probability p.i. Call rv again to make sure it’s now there.
    1. Finally, take the sum of weighteddevsq to get variance. Square root this to get standard deviation.

5.

The mean height of adults is 65 inches, with a standard deviation of 4 inches. Use the normal distribution to find the probabilities of the following scenarios:

    1. Find the probability of someone being at least 60 inches tall using pnorm().
    1. Find the probability of someone being at most 60 inches tall.
    1. Find the probability of someone being between 61 and 69 inches tall. Why is this number familiar?
    1. Find the probability of someone being between 57 and 73 inches tall. Why is this number familiar?
